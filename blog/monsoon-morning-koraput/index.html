A Monsoon Morning in Koraput

14 Aug 2025 Sanjukta Mishra 5 min read

The fog reaches Koraput before the sun does. By 5am the ridgelines of the Eastern Ghats have already dissolved into a soft grey nothing, and the town below wakes to a hush that feels older than the buildings around it. This is the hour our guides love best — not for what you can see, but for what you can hear before you can see it.

Somewhere past the tea stalls, a haat is assembling itself out of the mist. These weekly markets — held on rotating days across the villages of Koraput, Kundra and Semiliguda — are where the tribal communities of the region have traded, argued, matched-made and caught up on news for generations. Dongria Kondh women arrive first, carrying baskets of forest greens, wild tubers and hand-pounded turmeric balanced on their heads with an ease that makes the whole climb up look effortless.

By 7am the haat is a small city. Millet and mahua liquor sit beside cheap plastic toys and steel utensils — the old economy and the new one sharing the same patch of red earth. There's no signage, no fixed layout, no attempt to perform "authenticity" for anyone. It simply is what it has always been, and that's precisely why it's worth slowing down for.

“The haat rewards patience — sit a little longer, and the market stops being a sight and starts being a place.”

We don't rush our travelers through haat mornings. The instinct with any market is to "see everything" in twenty minutes and move on — but a haat rewards the opposite. Sit at the tea stall a little longer. Watch how a price is settled without a word of English or Odia, just a nod and an exchange of hands. Ask, through your guide, what a particular root is called and how it's cooked — you'll usually get an answer, and often an invitation.

What stays with visitors long after isn't a photograph — though the light is extraordinary — it's the quiet reminder that Odisha's tribal belt isn't a museum piece. It's a living, working, bargaining, laughing economy that has simply chosen to keep doing things its own way, mist or no mist.
